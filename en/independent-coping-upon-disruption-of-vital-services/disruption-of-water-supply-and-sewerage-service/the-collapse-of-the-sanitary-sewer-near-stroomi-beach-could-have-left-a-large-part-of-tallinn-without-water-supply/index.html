    As it happened

    The collapse of the sanitary sewer near Stroomi beach could have left a large part of Tallinn without water supply

    Back to the guidelines

    In October 2014, Estonia was on the verge of a crisis of the extent that had never been seen in the country, when, after the collapse of a sanitary sewer there was a real impending danger that up to three quarters of Tallinn city would have lost water supply and sewerage services.

    The events began to unravel on late 22 October, when the city water supply network operator AS Tallinna Vesi informed the rescue services that an area of almost 30 metres in diameter near Stroomi beach had caved in by almost one metre, and the reason was critical. Cracks and ridges had appeared along the underground sanitary sewer below, and they were increasing in size so fast that the sewer could collapse. The structure known as Tihase sewer is a major sewerage hub 2.3 metres in diameter, which brings sewage and some of surface runoff from the city’s Kristiine and Mustamäe districts to Paljassaare sewage treatment plant.

    In a worst-case scenario, if the sewer had stopped functioning, it would have resulted in extensive environmental pollution, wastewater accumulation in the streets and loss of drinking and domestic water supply in numerous districts of Tallinn. Had the sanitary sewer collapsed, there would have been a flood of sewage in the streets and basements of Kristiine and Mustamäe. To stop it, water supply would have to be cut off in the districts with a total population of over 100,000, dozens of schools and kindergartens and three major medical facilities: Mustamäe and Merimetsa hospitals as well as the Meremeeste polyclinic. In addition, there was tangible threat that the collapse of the Mustamäe and Kristiine sewer could have damaged another sanitary sewer nearby, which would have disrupted sewage removal from Haabersti and Nõmme city districts and partially affected the small town of Tabasalu.

    Fortunately, this time the situation was handled effectively. In accordance with much-enacted crisis plans, Tallinna Vesi started rerouting sewage from Mustamäe and Kristiine to other city districts, bypassing Tihase sewer; a substantial fleet of pumps was brought to the site, and more were ordered from Belgium, Great Britain and Latvia. Scientists and engineers from Estonia and abroad were summoned for assistance. Although the top of the sanitary sewer kept sinking in further for ten more days, it did not collapse entirely.

    Although this case was not reported to the public while it was taking place, it was a wake-up call for the providers of vital services to make sure they had a comprehensive overview of the condition of their infrastructures and played out various risk scenarios which might one day become reality.

    For common people, this incident illustrates that you must be aware of the risks in your area. It is also important to know what exactly is happening in case of various service outages. There has been plenty of information and explaining about such topics as fires and traffic accidents lately, but it is equally important to know what consequences the loss of drinking water supply can cause, for example, and what people personally could do to minimise them.

    What to do in a crisis

    Every state does whatever it can to prevent or respond to crisis situations. Still, help will not always reach all people quickly enough because some crises can affect a large part of the population and last for days or even weeks.

    Until help arrives and services are resumed, your welfare and that of your loved ones will largely depend on your own preparedness.

    The web page “Kriis.ee” contains tips on how to get prepared for various types of crisis situations and what to do if there actually is one.
